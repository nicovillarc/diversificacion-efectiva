## 9. Conclusions

The research question was: to what extent does the number of assets in a portfolio reflect its effective degree of diversification?

For the 60/40 portfolio analyzed (2016-01-01 to 2026-06-30), the empirical evidence indicates that the nominal count is an insufficient measure.

1. **Capital.** With \(N = 10\), the weight-based effective number is 9.55: no material capital concentration is observed from the target weights.
2. **Dependence.** Correlations are elevated within equities and within fixed income, and low across classes: ten tickers do not equal ten independent behaviors.
3. **Risk.** The 60/40 capital split does not match the risk split: equities account for 97.65% of total risk contribution (Euler decomposition); TSLA, NVDA, and MU dominate PRC despite weights identical to the rest of the equity block.
4. **Effective positions.** Moving from capital to PRC, \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}\) falls from 9.55 to 6.58.
5. **Common sources.** PCA on the correlation matrix identifies few dominant dimensions: 3 components ≥80% and 5 ≥90% of joint variability, with PC1 aligned to the equity block and PC2 to fixed income.

**Conceptual conclusion.** Portfolio diversification cannot be assessed by counting assets alone. Diagnosis requires analyzing dependence among instruments, the distribution of risk contributions, and the common-factor structure that explains their joint behavior.

The study is diagnostic, not normative: it does not optimize the portfolio or prescribe an alternative allocation.
